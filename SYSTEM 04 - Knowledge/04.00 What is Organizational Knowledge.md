## Organizational Knowledge Definition

Organizational knowledge is the collective understanding, expertise, processes, and information that enable the organization to function and succeed. It is the organization's memory and capability.

**Core Principle**: Knowledge is strategic asset. Organizations that manage knowledge well make better decisions, learn faster, reduce mistakes, and retain capability through change.

## Types of Organizational Knowledge

### Explicit Knowledge

**Documented and transferable**:
- Written procedures and processes
- Policy documents
- Decision frameworks
- Technical documentation
- Meeting notes and decisions
- Strategic plans
- Financial data
- Customer information
- Market research

**Characteristics**:
- Can be written down
- Transferable
- Storable
- Shareable
- Structured

**Examples**:
- Sales process documentation
- Customer onboarding checklist
- Software architecture documentation
- Financial policies
- Communication standards

### Tacit Knowledge

**Not easily documented; experiential**:
- How-to knowledge ("know-how")
- Judgment and intuition
- Relationships and networks
- Cultural understanding
- Problem-solving approaches
- Lessons learned
- Best practices
- Unwritten norms

**Characteristics**:
- Resides in people's heads
- Experiential
- Hard to transfer
- Contextual
- Developed over time

**Examples**:
- Best practices for customer negotiation
- How to build relationships with key partners
- Judgment in hiring decisions
- Understanding of organizational culture
- Problem-solving approaches

### Procedural Knowledge

**How things get done**:
- Work processes
- Decision-making processes
- Communication protocols
- Meeting structures
- Planning processes
- Problem-solving procedures
- Escalation procedures

**Characteristics**:
- Action-oriented
- Sequence-based
- Event-triggered
- Repeatable
- Improvable

**Examples**:
- Hiring process steps
- Project delivery methodology
- Issue resolution process
- Planning cadence
- Meeting agendas

### Conceptual Knowledge

**Understanding and mental models**:
- Strategic thinking
- Market understanding
- Competitive dynamics
- Customer insights
- Industry trends
- Organizational vision
- Values and principles

**Characteristics**:
- Interpretive
- Systemic
- Strategic
- Evolving
- Framework-based

**Examples**:
- Understanding of competitive landscape
- Customer personas and behavior
- Strategic positioning
- Organizational purpose
- Market trends

### Relational Knowledge

**Relationships and networks**:
- Who knows what
- Who can help with what
- Relationships with customers and partners
- Organizational networks
- External connections
- Trust relationships
- Influence networks

**Characteristics**:
- People-based
- Connection-based
- Context-dependent
- Evolving
- Hard to document

**Examples**:
- Key customer relationships
- Technical expert identification
- Partner network
- Industry contacts
- Department collaborators

## Levels of Organizational Knowledge

### Individual Knowledge

**What individuals know**:
- Personal expertise
- Skills and capability
- Lessons learned
- Relationships
- Experience

**Risk**: Leaves with person; not captured

**Mitigation**: Document, mentor, knowledge transfer

### Team Knowledge

**What teams collectively know**:
- Shared understanding
- Team processes
- Team memory
- Collective learning
- Team culture

**Strength**: Redundancy; shared ownership

**Challenge**: Doesn't transfer to other teams easily

### Departmental Knowledge

**What department knows**:
- Functional expertise
- Process knowledge
- Customer/domain knowledge
- Specialized capabilities
- Departmental culture

**Strength**: Specialized depth

**Challenge**: Silos; limited cross-department sharing

### Organizational Knowledge

**What organization collectively knows**:
- Strategic understanding
- Best practices
- Organizational processes
- Customer insights
- Market position

**Strength**: Organization-wide capability

**Challenge**: Synthesis across departments; integration

### External Knowledge

**Knowledge outside organization**:
- Market trends and research
- Industry best practices
- Customer feedback
- Competitive intelligence
- Regulatory changes
- Technology advances

**Importance**: Informs strategy and adaptation

**Responsibility**: Scan, curate, integrate

## Knowledge Lifecycle

### Creation

**Knowledge is created when**:
- Someone learns something new
- Team discusses and decides
- Process is designed and tested
- Analysis is completed
- Decision is made
- Experience occurs
- Mistake is made and analyzed

**Owner**: Person/team creating knowledge

### Capture

**Knowledge must be captured when**:
- Important learning occurs
- Valuable lesson learned
- Process is established
- Decision is documented
- Innovation happens
- Problem is solved
- Experience could help others

**Owner**: Individual or team responsible

### Organization

**Knowledge must be organized so**:
- Others can find it
- It can be referenced
- Context is clear
- It's up-to-date
- It's accessible

**Owner**: Knowledge management function

### Sharing

**Knowledge must be shared with**:
- Those who need to know
- New employees (onboarding)
- Relevant teams
- Appropriate audiences
- Accessible format

**Owner**: Various (managers, knowledge managers)

### Application

**Knowledge must be applied when**:
- Similar situation arises
- Problem needs solving
- Decision needs making
- Work is executed
- Improvement is considered

**Owner**: Knowledge users

### Refinement

**Knowledge is refined when**:
- Outdated
- New learning happens
- Better approach found
- Feedback received
- Context changes

**Owner**: Original creator or current expert

### Archiving

**Knowledge is archived when**:
- No longer applicable
- Historical only
- Technology/process changes
- Better knowledge replaces

**Owner**: Knowledge management

## Knowledge and Organizational Performance

**Knowledge management supports**:
- Better decision-making
- Faster problem-solving
- Reduced mistakes
- Faster onboarding
- Continuity through transitions
- Innovation and improvement
- Competitive advantage

**Organizations that manage knowledge well perform better.**

## Knowledge Loss

### Risks

**Knowledge is lost when**:
- Employee leaves without transfer
- Institutional memory walks out door
- Lessons learned not captured
- Undocumented processes
- Poor knowledge organization
- Lack of redundancy
- Time passes and memory fades

**Cost**: Relearning, mistakes, inefficiency, lost capability

### Prevention

**Prevent knowledge loss through**:
- Systematic capture
- Documentation
- Knowledge sharing culture
- Mentoring and onboarding
- Exit interviews and transitions
- Redundancy
- Regular review

## Knowledge Management Importance

**In growing organization**:
- More people to onboard
- More processes to document
- Faster knowledge loss risk
- More complexity
- More coordination needed

**Knowledge management becomes increasingly critical as organization grows.**

## Knowledge and Culture

**Knowledge-sharing culture**:
- Values learning and improvement
- Celebrates lessons learned
- Documents decisions
- Shares expertise
- Asks "how can we remember this?"
- Invests in knowledge systems
- Rewards knowledge contribution

**Knowledge hoarding culture**:
- Information as power
- Competitive individuals
- Silos
- Knowledge leaves with people
- Repeated mistakes

**Culture determines knowledge management success.**
