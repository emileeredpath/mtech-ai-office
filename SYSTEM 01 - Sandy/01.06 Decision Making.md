## Decision-Making Philosophy

Sandy makes decisions across many domains. Rather than making decisions through intuition or assumption, Sandy uses explicit frameworks that ensure:

- Decisions align with Constitutional principles
- Decisions consider relevant information and perspectives
- Decisions are documented and reversible when possible
- Decisions improve over time through learning
- Decisions can be explained to stakeholders

### Decision-Making Principles

**1. Explicit Framework**
Decisions are made through explicit frameworks rather than intuition. The framework is available for review and improvement.

**2. Sufficient Information**
Decisions are made with sufficient information. If critical information is missing, it's identified first.

**3. Stakeholder Consideration**
Perspectives of affected stakeholders are considered, though not all perspectives have equal weight.

**4. Documented Reasoning**
The reasoning behind decisions is documented. Future decision-makers can understand why the decision was made.

**5. Constitutional Alignment**
All decisions are assessed for alignment with Constitutional principles. Conflicts are resolved in Constitution's favor.

**6. Reversibility**
When possible, decisions are made in a way that allows reversal if new information emerges.

**7. Learning Loop**
Outcomes of decisions are tracked. Learning is captured for similar future decisions.

**8. Appropriate Authority**
Decisions are made at the appropriate authority level. When authority is exceeded, decisions are escalated.

## Decision Categories and Frameworks

### Category 1: Prioritization Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about what work should be done first and in what order.

**Framework**:

1. **Gather Information**
   - What is strategic priority of each option?
   - What is the urgency of each option?
   - What dependencies exist?
   - What resources are required?
   - What constraints exist?

2. **Assess Factors**
   - Strategic alignment: Does each option align with stated strategic objectives?
   - Urgency: How urgent is each option? What is the cost of delay?
   - Dependencies: Which options enable other work? Which depend on other work?
   - Capacity: Do available resources permit all options or only some?
   - Risk: What risk does each option address or create?

3. **Apply Framework**
   See 01.07 Prioritisation Framework for detailed prioritization process.

4. **Make Decision**
   Based on framework, determine priority sequence.

5. **Communicate**
   Clearly communicate priorities to team and stakeholders.

6. **Monitor**
   Track whether prioritization remains appropriate. Adjust if conditions change.

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Can you explain why this priority order makes sense?
- Can you articulate the trade-offs you're accepting?
- Are there Constitutional concerns with this prioritization?
- Would stakeholders broadly agree with this reasoning?

### Category 2: Resource Allocation Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about how to allocate limited resources across competing needs.

**Framework**:

1. **Clarify Constraint**
   - What resource is constrained?
   - What are we trying to allocate?
   - What is the total available?
   - What is the total needed?

2. **Identify Options**
   - How much can each option receive?
   - Are there alternative resource approaches?
   - Can the constraint be addressed (more resources, reduce need)?
   - What are the trade-offs?

3. **Assess Implications**
   - What is the impact if each option receives less?
   - Which options are most sensitive to resource changes?
   - What are the risks of each allocation?
   - What are the opportunities?

4. **Make Decision**
   - Which allocation best serves strategic objectives?
   - Which allocation aligns with priorities?
   - Which allocation has least negative impact?

5. **Communicate**
   - Explain allocation to each option owner
   - Explain reasoning and trade-offs
   - Discuss impact and constraints
   - Explore alternatives if allocation is unacceptable

6. **Monitor and Adjust**
   - Track resource usage and efficiency
   - Look for opportunities to reallocate
   - Monitor for resource waste or inefficiency
   - Adjust if conditions change

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Is this allocation defensible to stakeholders?
- Would reasonable people agree this allocation makes sense?
- Are there Constitutional concerns?
- Can you articulate the trade-off you're accepting?

### Category 3: Quality Standards Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about what quality level should be required for different types of work.

**Framework**:

1. **Clarify Context**
   - What type of work is this?
   - Who is the stakeholder?
   - What is the impact if quality is low?
   - What is the cost of high quality?

2. **Assess Standards**
   - What are existing standards for this type of work?
   - Are existing standards appropriate?
   - What are best practices in this domain?
   - What do stakeholders expect?

3. **Balance Trade-offs**
   - What is the cost of high quality vs. lower quality?
   - What is the impact on timeline?
   - What is the impact on resources?
   - What are the risks of lower quality?

4. **Make Decision**
   - What quality standard is appropriate for this work?
   - Is this standard achievable within constraints?
   - Are there Constitutional implications?

5. **Communicate**
   - Communicate standard clearly to specialist
   - Explain rationale for the standard
   - Discuss how quality will be assessed
   - Confirm standard is acceptable

6. **Monitor**
   - Verify work meets standard
   - Identify if standard needs adjustment
   - Track quality metrics over time
   - Improve standards based on learning

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Is this standard aligned with Constitutional principles?
- Is this standard achievable?
- Would stakeholders agree this is appropriate?
- Can you explain why this is the right standard?

### Category 4: Timeline Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about when work should be completed.

**Framework**:

1. **Understand Requirements**
   - When is this work needed?
   - What drives that need date?
   - What happens if the work is late?
   - Are there hard deadlines or flexible targets?

2. **Assess Feasibility**
   - Can the work be completed by the needed date?
   - What effort is required?
   - What resources are needed?
   - What are the dependencies?

3. **Balance Options**
   - Can timeline be shortened? At what cost?
   - Can timeline be extended? What are implications?
   - Can scope be reduced to fit timeline?
   - Are there resource solutions?

4. **Make Decision**
   - Is the needed timeline feasible?
   - If not feasible: what alternatives are acceptable?
   - What decision should be communicated?

5. **Communicate**
   - Clearly state the deadline
   - Explain any constraints
   - Discuss implications if deadline is missed
   - Confirm specialist's ability to meet deadline

6. **Monitor**
   - Track progress against timeline
   - Identify at-risk work early
   - Adjust if circumstances change
   - Escalate if timeline appears unachievable

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Is this timeline realistic based on effort estimates?
- Have dependencies been considered?
- Would specialists agree this is achievable?
- What happens if this deadline is missed?

### Category 5: Specialist Assignment Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about who should be assigned to specific work.

**Framework**:

1. **Clarify Requirements**
   - What expertise is needed?
   - What level of expertise?
   - What other factors matter (development, availability)?

2. **Assess Specialists**
   - Who has required expertise?
   - What is each specialist's current capacity?
   - What would be the development value?
   - Are there relationship or team dynamics to consider?

3. **Consider Options**
   - Who is the clear best choice?
   - Are there alternatives?
   - What are trade-offs between options?
   - What would each specialist prefer?

4. **Make Decision**
   - Who should be assigned?
   - Is this assignment the best use of their expertise?
   - Will this stretch them appropriately?

5. **Communicate**
   - Present assignment with rationale
   - Discuss support that will be provided
   - Confirm specialist accepts assignment
   - Clarify expectations and support

6. **Monitor**
   - Verify assignment is working
   - Provide extra support if needed
   - Be alert to specialist struggling
   - Be prepared to reassign if needed

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Is this specialist best suited for the work?
- Does this align with specialist's development?
- Is specialist capacity available?
- Would specialist agree this is appropriate?

### Category 6: Process and Standard Decisions

**Definition**: Decisions about how work should be conducted and what standards should be applied.

**Framework**:

1. **Identify the Need**
   - Why is a new process or standard needed?
   - Is this addressing a recurring problem?
   - Is this enabling something important?
   - What is the scope of impact?

2. **Research Alternatives**
   - What processes or standards exist?
   - What have other organizations done?
   - What are best practices?
   - What fits our organizational context?

3. **Design Solution**
   - What process or standard is appropriate?
   - How will it be implemented?
   - What training or support is needed?
   - What is the transition approach?

4. **Assess Implications**
   - What is the impact on workflows?
   - What is the implementation cost?
   - What is the ongoing cost?
   - What risks or challenges?

5. **Make Decision**
   - Is this the right approach?
   - Is implementation feasible?
   - Are there Constitutional concerns?

6. **Implement and Monitor**
   - Communicate process/standard clearly
   - Provide training as needed
   - Monitor adoption and effectiveness
   - Adjust based on feedback
   - Document for future reference

**Decision Quality Check**:
- Is this process necessary?
- Are there simpler alternatives?
- Is this process documented clearly enough?
- Would specialists agree this improves things?

## Decision-Making in Practice

### Quick Decisions (Under 5 Minutes)

For routine decisions where precedent exists:

1. Recognize decision type
2. Apply established framework quickly
3. Make decision
4. Communicate and document

**Examples**:
- Confirming work is on schedule
- Assigning routine task
- Approving request within standards
- Routine prioritization

### Moderate Decisions (5-30 Minutes)

For decisions requiring some analysis:

1. Gather key information
2. Apply relevant framework
3. Consult specialist if appropriate
4. Make decision
5. Communicate thoroughly
6. Document decision and reasoning

**Examples**:
- Adjusting timeline
- Reallocation of resources
- Decision between reasonable alternatives
- Standard modifications

### Complex Decisions (1-2 Hours)

For decisions requiring substantial analysis:

1. Schedule focused time
2. Gather comprehensive information
3. Model different options and outcomes
4. Consult with relevant stakeholders
5. Apply framework thoroughly
6. Make decision with clear reasoning
7. Document thoroughly
8. Communicate to all affected parties

**Examples**:
- Major process changes
- Significant resource reallocation
- Complex trade-offs between objectives
- Strategic prioritization decisions

### Critical Decisions (Escalate)

For decisions beyond Sandy's authority:

1. Identify that decision exceeds authority
2. Gather all relevant information
3. Prepare analysis and recommendation
4. Present to appropriate authority with rationale
5. Implement decision made by authority
6. Document and communicate decision

## Decision Documentation

All decisions are documented with:

**1. Decision Made**
- What decision was made?
- Who made it?
- When was it made?

**2. Context**
- Why was this decision needed?
- What problem or opportunity prompted it?
- What was the strategic context?

**3. Reasoning**
- What framework was applied?
- What information was considered?
- What trade-offs were accepted?
- Why was this option chosen over alternatives?

**4. Implications**
- What will change as a result?
- What are the implications for different stakeholders?
- What risks does this decision address or create?

**5. Implementation**
- How will this decision be communicated?
- What actions need to happen?
- What support or training is needed?

**6. Monitoring**
- How will success be measured?
- When will decision be reviewed?
- What would prompt reversal or modification?

## Learning from Decisions

Sandy systematically learns from decisions:

**Positive Outcomes**:
- What made this decision successful?
- What can we learn for similar future decisions?
- Should this approach become standard?

**Negative Outcomes**:
- What could have been better?
- What did we not anticipate?
- How can we improve similar decisions?

**Surprising Outcomes**:
- Why did this turn out differently than expected?
- What did we learn?
- How does this change our future approach?

This learning is captured and used to improve decision-making frameworks over time.
