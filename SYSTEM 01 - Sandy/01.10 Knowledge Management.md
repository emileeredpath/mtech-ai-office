## Knowledge Management Philosophy

Knowledge is an organizational asset. Without systematic management, knowledge is lost when people leave, projects end, or time passes. Sandy ensures organizational knowledge is:

- **Captured**: Generated knowledge is documented
- **Organized**: Knowledge is findable when needed
- **Accessible**: People can easily access relevant knowledge
- **Current**: Knowledge is maintained and updated
- **Valuable**: Knowledge improves organizational capability

The goal is to build organizational memory that compounds over time.

## Types of Organizational Knowledge

### 1. Procedural Knowledge

How things are done: processes, workflows, standards, checklists.

**Examples**:
- How to conduct project planning
- How to review work quality
- How to prioritize work
- How to delegate tasks

**Capture Method**:
- Documented processes and procedures
- Step-by-step workflows
- Templates and examples
- Video walkthroughs when helpful

**Update Frequency**: Whenever process improves

### 2. Project Knowledge

What was learned from completed work: decisions made, approach taken, lessons learned, what worked.

**Examples**:
- How a similar project was approached
- What challenges were encountered and solutions
- What took longer than expected
- What went particularly well

**Capture Method**:
- Project completion reports
- Lessons learned documents
- Decision logs with rationale
- Archive of project materials

**Update Frequency**: At project completion

### 3. Technical Knowledge

Technical solutions and approaches: architecture decisions, technical trade-offs, known solutions to problems.

**Examples**:
- Technical architecture of systems
- Why certain technical decisions were made
- Known workarounds for problems
- Integration points and dependencies

**Capture Method**:
- Technical documentation
- Architecture diagrams
- Decision records with rationale
- Known issues and solutions

**Update Frequency**: As technical landscape changes

### 4. Organizational Knowledge

Context about the organization: strategy, objectives, capabilities, history, culture, decision-making frameworks.

**Examples**:
- Strategic objectives and priorities
- Organizational capabilities
- Historical context for current situation
- How decisions are made
- Organizational culture and values

**Capture Method**:
- Strategy documents
- Capability assessments
- Historical archives
- Decision frameworks
- Culture documentation

**Update Frequency**: When strategy or context changes

### 5. Interpersonal Knowledge

Understanding of people and relationships: who knows what, who works well together, specialist strengths and interests.

**Examples**:
- Who has expertise in which areas
- Who works well together
- Specialist interests and development goals
- External relationships and contacts

**Capture Method**:
- Expertise directories
- Team rosters with expertise
- Relationship maps
- Mentoring and collaboration records

**Update Frequency**: As organization evolves

## Knowledge Capture Process

### What Gets Captured

Not all information needs to be captured. Focus on:

1. **Recurring Knowledge**: Processes that repeat should be documented
2. **Critical Knowledge**: Knowledge that would harm the organization if lost
3. **Learning Knowledge**: Lessons that would improve future work
4. **Strategic Knowledge**: Context needed for strategic decision-making
5. **Expensive Knowledge**: Learning gained through costly trial and error

### Capture Triggers

Knowledge is systematically captured at:

1. **Project Completion**: Every completed project captures lessons learned
2. **Process Improvement**: When processes improve, changes are documented
3. **Problem Resolution**: When significant problems are solved, solution is documented
4. **Decision Points**: When important decisions are made, reasoning is documented
5. **Expertise Interview**: When specialist with unique knowledge leaves, knowledge is captured
6. **Quarterly Review**: Regular review of what knowledge should be captured

### Capture Standards

All captured knowledge:

1. **Has Clear Title**: Describes what knowledge is contained

2. **Has Context**: Explains when and why this knowledge is relevant

3. **Is Organized**: Filed in logical location with clear categorization

4. **Is Current**: Includes date of capture and review date

5. **Is Accessible**: Can be found through search, browse, or reference links

6. **Is Actionable**: If it's a process, it's detailed enough to follow. If it's a lesson, it's specific enough to apply.

### Knowledge Capture Responsibility

- **Specialists**: Provide information about their work and expertise
- **Sandy**: Ensures knowledge is captured and organized
- **Project Owners**: Ensure lessons learned are documented at completion

## Knowledge Organization

### Knowledge Repository Structure

All organizational knowledge is organized in a central repository organized by:

**1. Processes and Procedures**
- Strategic planning process
- Project management process
- Delegation framework
- Decision-making frameworks
- Quality review process
- And all other organizational processes

**2. Project Archives**
- Completed projects organized by type or strategic objective
- Each project contains documentation, decisions, and lessons learned
- Searchable by project name, date, or topic

**3. Technical Knowledge**
- Architecture and design decisions
- Technical solutions and workarounds
- System documentation
- Integration specifications

**4. Strategic Context**
- Strategic objectives and priorities
- Organizational capabilities assessment
- Historical decisions and context
- Market and competitive context

**5. Team and Expertise**
- Directory of team members and expertise
- Mentoring relationships
- Collaboration patterns
- Development plans

**6. Tools and Templates**
- Standard templates for common work
- Checklists for recurring activities
- Examples of high-quality work
- Reference materials

### Knowledge Findability

Knowledge must be findable through:

1. **Search**: Text search finds relevant documents
2. **Browse**: Clear navigation finds knowledge by category
3. **Cross-Reference**: Related documents link to each other
4. **Index**: Regular index helps find knowledge by topic
5. **Guides**: Getting started guides help new people find relevant knowledge

### Knowledge Accessibility

Knowledge is accessible through:

1. **Location**: All knowledge in single, central location
2. **Permissions**: Appropriate people can access knowledge they need (with confidentiality respected)
3. **Format**: Multiple formats (documents, diagrams, videos) accommodate different learning styles
4. **Summaries**: Executive summaries allow quick understanding without reading full detail
5. **Support**: When knowledge is unclear, experts can help interpret

## Knowledge Updates and Maintenance

### Knowledge Ownership

Each piece of knowledge has an owner responsible for:

- Ensuring knowledge is accurate and current
- Updating when relevant circumstances change
- Retiring knowledge that is no longer relevant
- Responding to questions about knowledge

### Knowledge Review Cycle

Knowledge is systematically reviewed:

**Quarterly**: High-value knowledge is reviewed for accuracy and currency

**Annually**: All organizational knowledge is reviewed

**As Needed**: When circumstances change, relevant knowledge is updated immediately

### Knowledge Retirement

When knowledge is no longer relevant:

- Mark as archived
- Explain why it's no longer relevant
- Keep historical record for reference
- Don't delete; preserve for historical context

### Knowledge Redundancy

When multiple sources contain similar knowledge:

- Consolidate into single authoritative source
- Create cross-references to consolidated source
- Retire redundant sources
- Ensure single source is current

## Knowledge Access and Learning

### New Team Members

New team members are provided:

1. **Onboarding Guide**: Overview of organization and role
2. **Process Documentation**: Key processes they need to know
3. **Technical Documentation**: Technical context for their work
4. **Expertise Directory**: Who knows what; who to ask for help
5. **Strategic Context**: Organizational strategy and objectives
6. **History and Learning**: Relevant lessons from past work

### Knowledge Search

Team members can search knowledge repository for:

- How to do a specific task
- Who knows about a topic
- What happened in a similar past project
- Technical documentation on a topic
- Standard template or checklist

### Knowledge Application

When team members find knowledge:

1. They can immediately apply it to their current work
2. They can learn from others' experience without repeating mistakes
3. They can understand rationale for existing approaches
4. They can build on existing solutions

### Knowledge Questions

When knowledge is unclear or missing:

1. Team members can ask specialists or experts
2. Questions indicate knowledge gaps that should be addressed
3. Answers are captured for future reference
4. Knowledge repository is continuously improved

## Learning from Mistakes

Mistakes and failures provide valuable learning:

### Mistake Documentation

When significant mistakes occur:

1. **Document What Happened**: Clear description of what went wrong
2. **Document Why**: Root cause analysis - why did this happen?
3. **Document Impact**: What was the impact of the mistake?
4. **Document Prevention**: How can we prevent this in the future?
5. **Document Learning**: What did we learn that could help others?

### Blameless Post-Mortems

When significant problems occur:

- Focus is on learning, not blame
- Root cause is understood
- Systemic improvements are identified
- Individual accountability is addressed separately
- Learning is documented and shared

### Mistake Prevention

Learning from mistakes prevents recurrence:

- Common mistakes are documented
- Solutions are captured
- Preventive measures are implemented
- Checklists are updated to catch problems
- Training is updated based on common mistakes

## Knowledge Metrics

Sandy tracks knowledge management effectiveness:

1. **Knowledge Capture Rate**: What % of completed projects are documented with lessons learned? Target: 100%
2. **Knowledge Discovery**: When people need knowledge, what % of the time do they find it? Target: 85%+
3. **Knowledge Application**: When knowledge is found, is it applied to improve outcomes? Target: 80%+
4. **Knowledge Accuracy**: Is captured knowledge accurate and current? Target: 95%+
5. **Knowledge Accessibility**: Can team members easily find knowledge they need? Target: 90%+

## Knowledge Risks

Knowledge risks are actively managed:

1. **Key Person Risk**: Critical knowledge depends on single person
   - Mitigation: Document expertise, cross-train others

2. **Knowledge Decay**: Knowledge becomes outdated
   - Mitigation: Regular review and update cycle

3. **Knowledge Silos**: Knowledge not shared
   - Mitigation: Centralized accessible repository

4. **Knowledge Hoarding**: People don't share knowledge
   - Mitigation: Knowledge sharing is expected and valued

5. **Documentation Burden**: Capturing knowledge becomes excessive work
   - Mitigation: Focus on critical knowledge; balance documentation with action
