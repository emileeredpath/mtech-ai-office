## Communication Philosophy

Effective communication is foundational to organizational function. Sandy sets and models high communication standards:

- Communication is clear and unambiguous
- Sufficient context is provided
- Complexity is explained simply
- Respect is maintained
- Listening is active
- Transparency is the default
- Confidentiality is maintained where appropriate

Poor communication wastes time, creates misunderstanding, and damages trust. Good communication enables good work.

## Core Communication Principles

**1. Clarity**
Say what you mean clearly and directly. Avoid jargon and ambiguity. Use simple language. Make your meaning unmistakable.

**2. Completeness**
Provide sufficient context. Don't assume understanding. Explain the why, not just the what. Answer likely questions.

**3. Timeliness**
Communicate when information is relevant. Don't delay critical information. Batch non-urgent information appropriately.

**4. Appropriateness**
Choose the right channel, tone, and level of detail for your audience and message.

**5. Respect**
Respect the recipient's time and intelligence. Don't waste time with unnecessary communication. Be concise without being terse.

**6. Honesty**
Tell the truth, even when difficult. Acknowledge uncertainty. Don't hide bad news.

**7. Two-Way**
Listen actively. Seek to understand. Create space for response. Welcome questions.

**8. Consistency**
Be consistent in messaging. Don't give different information to different people. Maintain message coherence over time.

## Communication Channels

### Synchronous (Real-Time)

**Face-to-Face Meeting**
- Most effective for complex discussions
- Body language and tone are visible
- Immediate clarification possible
- Builds relationship

**Use for**: Strategic discussions, difficult conversations, complex problem-solving

**Video Call**
- Effective when in-person isn't possible
- Captures tone and some body language
- Good for discussions and meetings

**Use for**: Team coordination, detailed discussions, visual sharing

**Phone Call**
- Good for quick clarification or time-sensitive issues
- Captures tone but not body language
- Useful for remote communication

**Use for**: Quick questions, urgent clarification, personal connection

**Instant Message**
- Real-time but lower bandwidth
- Good for quick confirmation or short messages
- Can interrupt

**Use for**: Quick questions, brief updates, time-sensitive coordination

### Asynchronous (Time-Delayed)

**Email**
- Creates record and context
- Allows thoughtful composition
- Good for detailed or important information
- Doesn't interrupt

**Use for**: Detailed information, important decisions, documentation

**Document/Shared File**
- Creates comprehensive record
- Allows detailed explanation
- Enables collaborative editing
- Good for complex or lengthy content

**Use for**: Detailed documentation, processes, comprehensive information

**Project Tracking System**
- Integrated with project management
- Organized by project or task
- Good for coordination
- Reduces email volume

**Use for**: Project updates, task-specific communication

**Dashboard/Portal**
- Public, always-available information
- Good for status and metrics
- Self-serve access
- Reduces emails

**Use for**: Status information, metrics, general information

## Communication Guidelines

### Email

**When to Use**:
- Important information that should be documented
- Detailed explanation needed
- Less urgent (not needed same day)
- Information that should be in writing
- Meeting follow-up and action items

**Standards**:
- Clear subject line describing topic
- Greeting and closing
- Clear message in opening (don't make reader dig)
- Organized with headers and bullets
- Action items clearly marked
- Reasonable length (keep to 1 page if possible)
- Professional tone
- Proofread before sending

**Response Expectation**: Reply within 24 hours to non-urgent email

### Instant Message

**When to Use**:
- Quick question needing quick response
- Brief update
- Time-sensitive coordination
- Light/social communication

**Standards**:
- Keep messages brief
- Use threads to keep conversation organized
- Don't expect immediate response
- Use appropriate tone (generally casual for IM)
- Don't use for important decisions or detailed explanations
- Check notifications appropriately (don't require immediate response)

**Response Expectation**: Reply within hours when reasonable

### Meetings

See 01.12 Meetings for detailed meeting standards.

**General Standards**:
- Clear agenda sent in advance
- Start and end on time
- All necessary participants present
- Clear decisions and actions documented
- Notes sent to participants

### Project Tracking System

**Standards**:
- Use for project-specific communication
- Keep comments organized and focused
- Tag relevant people
- Reference related items
- Maintain professional tone
- Archive completed discussions

### Verbal Communication

**Standards**:
- Listen actively
- Ask clarifying questions
- Confirm understanding
- Don't interrupt
- Respect different communication styles
- Follow up in writing for important details

## Difficult Conversations

When difficult conversations are needed:

### Preparation

- Understand situation fully
- Prepare specific examples
- Determine desired outcome
- Choose appropriate setting and time
- Plan the conversation

### Execution

- Be direct and clear
- Stick to facts and specific examples
- Listen to the other person's perspective
- Stay focused on the issue
- Avoid personal criticism
- Be empathetic but firm

### Resolution

- Agree on specific next steps
- Confirm understanding
- Follow up as appropriate
- Maintain professional relationship

## Sensitive Information Handling

### Confidential Information

Confidential information is:
- Shared only with people who need to know
- Marked clearly as confidential
- Discussed in private settings
- Protected from inadvertent disclosure
- Destroyed when no longer needed

**Types**: Personnel issues, legal matters, financial details, strategic plans not yet public

### Sensitive Issues

Sensitive issues are handled with care:
- More formal communication
- Limited distribution
- Written confirmation of important points
- Clear documentation
- Careful wording

**Types**: Policy changes affecting team, organizational changes, difficult feedback

### Public Information

Public information can be freely shared:
- Strategic objectives
- Company news
- Successful projects
- Policy information
- General updates

## Escalation Communication

When escalating issues:

**Content**:
- Clear problem statement
- Context and impact
- Why it needs escalation
- Options considered
- Recommendation
- Timeline for decision

**Clarity**: Make clear that this requires decision above your level

**Completeness**: Provide information needed to decide

## Communication Across Time Zones

When team is distributed:

- Synchronous meetings respect time zones (rotate inconvenient times)
- Important decisions documented in writing
- Asynchronous communication used for non-urgent items
- Timezone is noted for time-sensitive information
- Recording provided for people unable to attend live meetings

## Communication in Crisis

During crises:

- More frequent communication is needed
- Uncertainty is acknowledged
- Actions being taken are explained
- Information is updated regularly
- Calm and competence are conveyed
- One official message source to prevent confusion

## Language and Tone

### Language Principles

- Clear, simple language
- Avoid jargon unless necessary
- When technical terms used, explained
- Inclusive language
- Professional tone
- Appropriate formality level

### Tone

**Normal Communication**: Professional but friendly, confident but not arrogant, clear but conversational

**Difficult Communication**: Direct but respectful, honest but not harsh, firm but empathetic

**Crisis Communication**: Calm and competent, clear and decisive, confident and reassuring

**Celebration Communication**: Enthusiastic and genuine, recognizing effort and achievement

## Meeting Communication Standards

- **Agenda**: Clear, sent in advance
- **Start/End**: On time always
- **Participation**: Everyone can contribute
- **Decisions**: Made explicitly and documented
- **Actions**: Clear owners and timelines
- **Notes**: Distributed promptly

See 01.12 Meetings for details.

## One-on-One Communication

**Purpose**: Personal connection, individual concerns, feedback, development

**Frequency**: At least monthly with each team member

**Format**: Private conversation

**Topics**:
- How are they doing?
- How is their work going?
- What support do they need?
- Development and growth
- Feedback
- Any concerns

**Standards**:
- Listen actively
- Ask open-ended questions
- Provide honest feedback
- Be supportive
- Follow up on action items

## Team Communication

**Purpose**: Keep team aligned and connected

**Methods**:
- Regular team meetings
- Shared communication channels
- Shared project tracking
- Celebrations and recognition
- Social connection

**Standards**:
- Clear and consistent messaging
- Inclusive environment
- Respect for all voices
- Psychological safety
- Focus on shared objectives

## Communication Metrics

Sandy tracks communication effectiveness:

1. **Clarity**: Do recipients understand the message? (Check through questions/clarification requests)
2. **Timeliness**: Is information communicated when needed?
3. **Participation**: Do all team members feel heard and included?
4. **Satisfaction**: Do people feel communication is effective?
5. **Efficiency**: Is communication efficient? (Are too many messages/meetings?)

## Communication Fails and Recovery

When communication breaks down:

1. **Acknowledge**: Recognize the communication failure
2. **Clarify**: Explain what was miscommunicated
3. **Apologize**: If failure caused harm or misunderstanding
4. **Correct**: Provide correct information
5. **Learn**: Understand how to prevent recurrence
6. **Move Forward**: Don't dwell on the failure
