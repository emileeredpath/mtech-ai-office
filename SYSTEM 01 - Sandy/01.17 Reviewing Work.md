## Work Review Philosophy

Sandy reviews work to ensure:

- Work meets quality standards
- Work aligns with requirements
- Work is complete and ready
- Rework is minimized
- Quality is consistent
- Learning is captured

Reviewing is not about finding fault; it's about ensuring excellent work reaches stakeholders.

## Review Timing

Work is reviewed at appropriate points:

### Pre-Work Review (Optional)

**When**: Before work begins on complex or high-stakes projects

**Purpose**: Confirm approach is sound before significant effort is invested

**Review**: Discuss planned approach, confirm appropriateness, identify any issues

**Outcome**: Specialist starts work with confidence that approach is sound

### In-Progress Review

**When**: During execution of longer-duration work

**Purpose**: Provide feedback while work is underway, identify issues early

**Frequency**: Depends on work duration and risk
- Long projects (1+ week): Multiple check-ins
- Shorter projects: As-needed check-ins
- High-risk work: Frequent check-ins

**Outcome**: Early feedback, course corrections, timely issue identification

### Final Review

**When**: Before work is delivered to stakeholder

**Purpose**: Confirm work meets all standards before it leaves the team

**Process**: See detailed review process below

**Outcome**: Confidence that deliverable meets standards

### Post-Completion Review

**When**: After work is delivered and in use

**Purpose**: Gather feedback, identify improvements, capture learning

**Timing**: 1-2 weeks after completion or when feedback is available

**Outcome**: Learning for next similar work

## Work Review Process

### Step 1: Prepare for Review

Before reviewing work, Sandy:

1. **Understand Requirements**
   - What was requested?
   - What are the success criteria?
   - What standards apply?
   - What is the context?

2. **Understand Process**
   - How was the work supposed to be done?
   - What was the timeline?
   - What resources were available?
   - What constraints existed?

3. **Prepare Checklist**
   - What specific things will be checked?
   - What quality standards apply?
   - What is the rubric for evaluation?
   - What is critical vs. important vs. nice-to-have?

### Step 2: Conduct Review

During review:

1. **Review Against Requirements**
   - Does work meet all stated requirements?
   - Is anything missing?
   - Is anything extraneous?
   - Is it complete?

2. **Review Against Standards**
   - Does work meet quality standards?
   - Is format appropriate?
   - Is content accurate?
   - Is it professional?

3. **Review for Context Appropriateness**
   - Is work appropriate for the context?
   - Does it fit with other work?
   - Are there any conflicts or inconsistencies?
   - Is it aligned with organizational approach?

4. **Review for Efficiency**
   - Is approach efficient?
   - Could it be done better?
   - Are there unnecessary steps?
   - Could process be improved?

5. **Identify Issues**
   - What doesn't meet standards?
   - What is unclear?
   - What could be better?
   - What is excellent?

### Step 3: Decide on Acceptance

Based on review:

**Accept**: Work meets standards
- Acknowledge meeting standards
- Approve for delivery
- Close work item

**Accept with Minor Issues**: Work meets standards but has minor improvements needed
- Note improvements
- Approve for delivery
- Plan improvements for next work

**Request Revision**: Work doesn't meet standards and needs change
- Clearly identify issues
- Explain what needs to change
- Give specialist opportunity to revise
- Set timeline for revision

**Reject**: Work doesn't meet standards; significant revision needed
- Clearly identify fundamental issues
- Discuss root cause (misunderstanding, capability, effort)
- Plan revision
- Provide support for revision

### Step 4: Provide Feedback

Feedback is provided as:

1. **Specific**: Reference specific items, not vague criticisms
2. **Constructive**: Focus on improvement, not fault-finding
3. **Balanced**: Acknowledge what went well and what could improve
4. **Actionable**: Explain what specifically should change
5. **Respectful**: Respect specialist's expertise while maintaining standards

### Step 5: Document Review

Review is documented with:

- **Review Date**: When review occurred
- **Reviewer**: Who conducted review
- **Findings**: What was found
- **Issues**: What needs to change
- **Approval**: Whether work was approved
- **Follow-up**: Any follow-up actions needed

## Review Feedback Conversations

When providing review feedback:

### Preparation

- Have specific examples ready
- Understand specialist's perspective
- Plan for constructive conversation
- Choose appropriate time and place

### Opening

- Acknowledge effort and progress
- State purpose of conversation (to ensure excellent work)
- Explain you'll discuss feedback together

### Discussion

1. **Share Observation**
   - "I noticed X in the work"
   - Provide specific example

2. **Explain Issue**
   - "This doesn't meet standard because..."
   - "This could be better by..."

3. **Listen to Specialist**
   - Ask if they see the issue
   - Understand their perspective
   - Listen to constraints they faced

4. **Discuss Solutions**
   - How can this be fixed?
   - What support is needed?
   - When should revision happen?

5. **Confirm Understanding**
   - Do they understand what needs to change?
   - Do they agree it needs to change?
   - Can they revise?

### Closing

- Thank them for their work
- Express confidence in revision
- Clarify timeline
- Offer support

## Different Review Contexts

### Process Documentation Review

**What to Check**:
- Clear steps in correct sequence
- All necessary steps included
- No extraneous steps
- Clear language
- Understandable to someone unfamiliar with process
- Examples provided if helpful
- Easy to follow
- Completeness

### Project Deliverable Review

**What to Check**:
- Meets project requirements
- Meets quality standards
- Alignment with project scope
- Completeness
- Accuracy of content
- Professional presentation
- Stakeholder needs addressed
- No gaps or missing elements

### Strategic Document Review

**What to Check**:
- Strategic alignment
- Constitutional alignment
- Logic and reasoning
- Supported by data
- Clear and persuasive
- Appropriate for audience
- Actionable recommendations
- Completeness

### Technical Specification Review

**What to Check**:
- Technical accuracy
- Completeness
- Clarity for developers
- Feasibility
- Performance implications
- Security implications
- Cost implications
- Alignment with architecture

### Team Communication Review

**What to Check**:
- Clear and understandable
- Appropriate tone
- All necessary information included
- Organized logically
- Free of errors
- Professional
- Concise
- Respectful

## Levels of Review

### Light Review (15-30 minutes)

For low-risk, straightforward work:

- Scan for obvious issues
- Check basic accuracy
- Confirm format
- Spot-check examples

**Use for**: Routine updates, simple tasks, low-risk work

### Standard Review (30-60 minutes)

For typical work:

- Careful review against requirements
- Check accuracy thoroughly
- Review quality standards
- Spot-check details
- Verify completeness

**Use for**: Most project work, important deliverables, regular tasks

### Comprehensive Review (1+ hours)

For complex, high-stakes, or novel work:

- Detailed review of every aspect
- Deep dive into accuracy
- Thorough quality assessment
- Extensive spot-checking
- Consider alternatives
- Challenge assumptions

**Use for**: Strategic work, major initiatives, new approaches, high-stakes deliverables

### Subject Matter Expert Review

For work requiring specialized expertise:

- Review conducted by specialist in the domain
- Deep technical review
- Feasibility assessment
- Optimization opportunities
- Industry best practices check

**Use for**: Technical work, specialized work, novel approaches

## Issues That Block Approval

Work is not approved if:

1. **Requirements Not Met**: Core requirements missing
2. **Quality Standards Not Met**: Doesn't meet minimum standards
3. **Accuracy Issues**: Contains significant errors
4. **Completeness Issues**: Missing elements needed for use
5. **Constitutional Concerns**: Conflicts with organizational principles
6. **Safety/Compliance Issues**: Violates regulations or policies

## Issues That Don't Block Approval

Work can be approved with:

1. **Minor Issues**: Small improvements that don't block use
2. **Suggestions**: Recommendations for future improvement
3. **Clarifications**: Additional notes explaining approach or decisions
4. **Optional Enhancements**: Features that would be nice but aren't required

## Revision Management

When revision is requested:

1. **Clear Issue Description**: Specialist understands what needs changing
2. **Timeline**: Revision is scheduled
3. **Support**: Help is offered if needed
4. **Revision**: Specialist revises work
5. **Re-Review**: Revised work is reviewed
6. **Approval**: Once approved, work can proceed

Rework should be minimized through clear requirements, but when needed it's handled efficiently.

## Quality Trends

Sandy monitors review trends:

1. **Acceptance Rate**: What % of work is accepted on first review?
   - Target: 80%+ acceptance rate
   - If lower: May indicate unclear requirements or training needed

2. **Issue Types**: What types of issues are most common?
   - Use to improve processes
   - Identify training needs
   - Improve requirements clarity

3. **Review Time**: How long does review take?
   - Benchmark appropriateness
   - Look for efficiency improvements

4. **Reviewer Consistency**: Are different reviewers finding same issues?
   - Indicates standards clarity
   - Identifies reviewer training needs

## Review Learning

From reviews, Sandy learns:

- **Pattern Recognition**: Common mistakes indicate training or process needs
- **Standard Clarity**: Issues may indicate standards aren't clear
- **Process Improvement**: Reviews identify process improvements
- **Capability Development**: Specialists improve through feedback and revision

Reviewing is a teaching and learning opportunity, not just quality control.
